A Letter From Winston

Why I'm Sharing This

This is my third year at Abundance360. I go because I want to be around people who are genuinely looking around the corner — entrepreneurs, scientists, investors, and thinkers who are building toward the future rather than away from it. That energy is rare. And every year I leave feeling a responsibility I can't ignore: to share what I've learned with the people I care about. That's all this is. I'm not here to tell you what to do. I'm a witness, and I want to tell you what I saw.

The honest description of that room this year is: electric, optimistic, and scared — all at the same time. Not scared in a paralyzed way. Scared in the way you feel when you're standing at the edge of something enormous and you understand, fully, that there is no going back. The people in that room — some of the most accomplished builders and thinkers I've encountered — were genuinely grappling with what comes next. That combination of excitement and honest fear was the most striking thing I brought home.

Here is what I heard. AI has crossed from tool to collaborator — this year, agents can act on your behalf around the clock, not just answer questions. Humanoid robots are two to three years from mass deployment; speakers projected roughly a billion operating in society within five years. David Sinclair told the room he is "days away" from the first human trial of epigenetic reprogramming — a process that could reverse aging at the cellular level. Peter Diamandis described "longevity escape velocity," the point where science extends your life faster than time passes, and argued we are approaching it. Eric Schmidt testified to Congress about a 92-gigawatt power shortage in the US through 2030, driven largely by AI's energy demands. These are not predictions. They are things happening now, or in the very near term, reported by the people building them.

Bill Gross closed the conference with something that stayed with me: "The greatest risk right now is not AI. It's not turning to AI." He meant it as an invitation — use AI as a mirror, a thinking partner, a way to see what you're missing. I believe that. And I also believe something else that I want to say plainly.

We need smart guardrails — and we need them now. Not just on AI, but on the full range of what's coming: robotics, longevity, energy, security, education. The technology being built is extraordinary. The question of who benefits from it is not settled. I believe strongly that we deserve to demand transparency, accountability, and outcomes that serve society broadly — not just those who are first to move or best positioned to capture the gains. Eric Schmidt himself said we may need a "Chernobyl-style event" before regulation catches up. I hope he's wrong. But whether he is or isn't, that conversation belongs to all of us — not just governments and tech companies. Our voices matter here.

I came home holding both things at once: genuine excitement about what this moment makes possible, and a clear-eyed belief that how it goes depends on whether enough people are paying attention and asking for accountability. The opportunity is real. So is the responsibility. Everything in this report is what I actually heard and learned. Take it for what it is — notes from someone who had a seat in the room, shared with people I care about.

Winston Fisher

The State of AI · 2026

We Have Crossed a Threshold

This is not incremental progress. The pace of change has reached a point where waiting to engage is itself a strategy — a losing one.

"We are at the knee of the exponential curve. The next 18 months will produce more change than the last 10 years combined." — Peter Diamandis, Abundance360, 2026

I spent this week surrounded by some of the most forward-thinking minds in business, science, and technology at Abundance360 — Peter Diamandis's annual gathering of people committed to understanding and shaping the future. What I heard was not hype. It was a sober, data-driven, and at times uncomfortable look at what is already happening — and what is coming next.

My honest takeaway: There is disruption coming that will touch every industry. But disruption has always created winners. The difference between prospering and struggling in the next three years comes down to one question: Are you on the side of the change, or the side being changed?
2023–2024
The Conversation Phase
AI could talk to you, write for you, answer questions. Impressive, but every action still required a human to take it somewhere. Useful as a tool, limited as a collaborator.
2025
AI Learns to Reason
Models like OpenAI o3, Google Gemini 2.0, and Anthropic Claude 3.7 Sonnet gained the ability to think through complex problems. They began outperforming human experts in medicine, law, and engineering on standardized benchmarks. This was the moment the tool became a collaborator.
2026 — Now
The Year of AI Agents
AI can now take actions, not just answer questions. Agents browse the web, write and execute code, manage workflows, book meetings, analyze documents, and complete multi-step tasks on your behalf. This is the year companies begin deploying AI workforces — not pilots, real operations. A single human can now direct what previously required a team of ten.
2027 — The Separation
Winners Emerge. Losers Become Visible.
AI-native businesses will operate at 3–5× the productivity of traditional competitors. The first measurable wave of white-collar job displacement will appear in economic data. New categories of work will emerge — but the transition will be painful for those who are unprepared. This is my prediction, not a certainty — but the trend lines are clear.
2028–2030
Physical AI and Abundance
Humanoid robots (Tesla Optimus, Figure, 1X) move from factories into service roles. AI-driven drug discovery produces major health breakthroughs. Longevity science becomes actionable. The cost of intelligence, labor, and computation continues its collapse. For those positioned on the right side of this curve, genuine abundance becomes accessible.
The Defining Concept · 2026

What Is an AI Agent?

This term will define the next two years. Understanding it will help you see both the opportunity and the threat clearly.

💬

Old AI: The Chatbot

You ask a question. AI answers. You copy the answer and do something with it. Every action still required a human. Useful — but the leverage was limited. You were the worker; AI was the assistant.

⚡

New AI: The Agent

You give AI a goal. It breaks the task into steps, takes actions on your behalf — reads documents, searches the web, writes and runs code, sends emails, updates systems — and reports back. You are the director. AI is the workforce.

🔗

Multi-Agent Systems

Companies are now deploying teams of AI agents that work in parallel: one researches, one writes, one checks compliance, one executes. A single human can direct the output of what once required a department.

What this means practically right now: A real estate analyst using AI agents today can do the work of five analysts. A financial advisor with AI tools can serve three times the clients. A solo entrepreneur can run operations that previously required a full team. The leverage is real, it is available today, and it costs less than one employee's monthly salary to access.
🦾

A360 Day 1 · Alex Finn / OpenClaw: From Chatbot to Employee

Jensen Huang called what's happening "the single most important release of software, probably ever." Alex Finn presented OpenClaw — open source, free — and framed why most people are still using AI completely wrong. His diagnosis: current AI has three fundamental gaps.

NO GROWTH

Same capability on day 1,000 as day 1. Doesn't learn from its own work. Doesn't build skills. Doesn't get smarter.

NO BODY

Can't use your computer. Can't browse the web. Can't send messages. All brain, no hands.

NO AGENCY

Never takes initiative. Only responds when asked. Doesn't monitor. Doesn't scan. Purely reactive.

OpenClaw's answer: Self-improving (creates its own skills, updates its knowledge), Body (uses your computer, browses, codes, manages files), Agency (works 24/7, monitors, acts without being asked). His analogy: "This is like buying a Ferrari and only using it to listen to the radio." The shift from AI as Tool to AI as Worker is not incremental — it is a category change.

Day 2 · Physical AI

The Robot Wave: Civilizational Scale, Starting Now

Humanoid robots aren't a 2030 story. Three companies presented at Abundance360 that are shipping commercially today. Here is what they actually said — and what it means for every business that depends on physical labor.

"The labor economics are already decided. The question is which businesses will be positioned on the right side of them." — A360 Day 2 · Robotics Session
🤖

1X Technologies · Vikram Kothari
Neo Humanoid Robot · Taking Commercial Orders Now

1X's Neo robot is designed for real-world commercial deployment at a target labor cost of approximately $10/hour — versus $25–35/hour for a loaded human worker in comparable roles. 1X is already accepting commercial orders. Vikram Kothari projected roughly one billion humanoid robots operating throughout society within five years. That is a bold forecast — but it comes from someone building the first wave of them, with production commitments in hand.

The number to anchor on: $10/hr robot vs. $25–35/hr loaded human. Run that math across your facilities, logistics, hospitality, or elder care operations. The unit economics are not theoretical — they are the current price.
📦

Agility Robotics · Peggy Johnson
Digit Robot · Amazon Partnership · Working in Warehouses Today

Agility's Digit is not a prototype — it is working inside Amazon fulfillment centers today. CEO Peggy Johnson (former Microsoft EVP) presented a company that has solved the deployment problem: Digit is LLM-agnostic, meaning it can be updated with better AI models without hardware changes. The safety-first architecture allows it to operate alongside humans. She is running more AV-style autonomous operations than anyone realizes — the physical AI revolution is already in the warehouses that stock your house.

Key design principle: LLM-agnostic means the robot gets smarter every year without being replaced. The hardware you buy in 2025 runs 2030 intelligence. That changes the capital calculation entirely.
🧬

Clone Robotics · Dhanush Radhakrishnan
Synthetic Humans · Myo Fiber Artificial Muscles

Clone Robotics is building something different: not industrial robots, but synthetic humans — with proprietary Myo Fiber artificial muscles that replicate human muscle mechanics. The long-term vision is explicit: "If you're going to upload a human brain to silicon, you probably want to upload it into something that looks and moves like a human." This is a longer-arc play, but the muscle technology has near-term applications in prosthetics, rehabilitation, and human-robot collaboration environments where the uncanny valley matters.

Why this matters beyond the headline: Myo Fiber is a materials breakthrough. The same synthetic muscle technology that makes robots more human will transform prosthetics, exoskeletons, and surgical instruments.

The Constraint Nobody Is Talking About

The Energy Equation: AI Has a Power Problem

Every AI model, every agent, every humanoid robot runs on electricity. Eric Schmidt testified to Congress about a 92 gigawatt US power shortage through 2030. This is not a background issue — it is the physical constraint on everything else in this report.

"We are facing a 92 gigawatt US power shortage through 2030. I testified to Congress about this. The AI buildout is being throttled by electricity." — Eric Schmidt · Abundance360 2026
⚡

Why 92 GW Matters

A gigawatt powers roughly 750,000 homes. The US is short 92 of them through 2030 — driven almost entirely by the explosion in data center demand from AI training and inference. Every ChatGPT query, every agent run, every model retrain consumes electricity. The pace of AI deployment is outrunning the pace of power grid expansion by a significant margin. This creates a real constraint on how fast the AI revolution can actually proceed — and a massive opportunity for anyone solving it.

☀️

The Race to Power the Future

  • Fusion energy — Commonwealth Fusion presented at A360. Their approach uses high-temperature superconducting magnets to achieve commercially viable fusion. Timeline: first commercial plant in the early 2030s. This is no longer science fiction — it is an engineering and capital problem
  • Nuclear revival — data center operators are signing long-term PPAs with nuclear plants; Microsoft signed a 20-year agreement to restart Three Mile Island
  • Grid-scale storage — the intermittency problem with renewables is being addressed with battery storage at a scale that changes the equation
  • Distributed compute — Alex Finn's home office runs 2x Mac Studios (512GB RAM each) and an NVIDIA DGX Spark ($4,000) — a personal AI supercomputer on his desk. Local AI reduces cloud dependency and grid pressure
🏢

What This Means for Business Leaders

  • Power infrastructure is becoming a competitive moat — data centers, logistics facilities, and commercial buildings with reliable, high-capacity power will command premiums
  • Energy costs are now a first-order variable in AI strategy — not an IT budget line
  • Real estate adjacent to power-dense infrastructure (substations, generation assets) will be repriced
  • Companies that own or secure renewable energy agreements now will have a cost advantage that compounds as electricity demand rises
  • The energy constraint means AI deployment will be uneven — companies and geographies with power access move faster
The connection most people are missing: The robot wave, the agent revolution, and the longevity science boom all have the same upstream dependency — electricity. The companies that win the AI era will be the ones that secured their power stack. This is why energy is not a utility question — it is a strategy question.
Day 3 · Health & Longevity Sessions

Longevity: The Science Is Moving Faster Than You Think

Abundance360's third day focused entirely on the convergence of AI and longevity science — from epigenetic reprogramming to drug repurposing, brain-computer interfaces to synthetic biology. Here is what the speakers actually presented.

"Aging is not simply wear and tear — it is a loss of epigenetic information. And if it's an information problem, it may be an information solution." — David Sinclair, Harvard Medical School · Abundance360 Day 3
Peter Diamandis: Healthspan, Not Just Lifespan

Peter Diamandis opened Day 3 by reframing the entire longevity question: the goal is not simply more years, but more healthy years — healthspan vs. lifespan. He cited a clinical case where a metabolic intervention achieved 52% prediabetes reversal in 1.7 years. He introduced the concept of "longevity escape velocity" — the point at which science is extending your life faster than time is passing — and argued that we are rapidly approaching it. He also announced a new XPRIZE partnership with Jamie Justice focused on extending human healthspan.

🧬

David Sinclair: Aging as an Information Problem

Sinclair's central thesis: aging is a loss of epigenetic information — not an inevitable biological process but a correctable one. He presented OSK reprogramming (three of the four Yamanaka genes) as the mechanism to reset cellular clocks. He told the audience he is "days away" from the first human epigenetic reprogramming trial, targeting the eye. His current protocol includes resveratrol, NMN, metformin, rapamycin, and GLP-1 compounds, plus senolytics Novitoclax and dasatinib. He predicted longevity medicines will become "much bigger than GLP-1s" — and GLP-1s are already reshaping medicine. He referenced Fountain Life as an example of longevity-focused care in practice.

🔬

Lila Sciences: AI Science Factories

Geoff von Maltzahn presented Lila Sciences — what he called "AI science factories": infrastructure capable of autonomously designing and building new DNA molecules, RNA molecules, antibodies, peptides, and organic molecules. The implication: the bottleneck in biology is no longer human researchers — it is the speed of the physical lab. AI-driven automation is beginning to remove that constraint entirely, collapsing drug development timelines.

💊

Every Cure: 4,000 Drugs × 18,000 Diseases

David Fajgenbaum presented Every Cure's drug repurposing mission: systematically match 4,000 existing FDA-approved drugs against 18,000 diseases to find overlooked therapeutic applications. The platform generates 1,000 repurposing ideas per month and currently has 10 active programs. The key insight: we already have tools that may cure diseases in new contexts — we just haven't looked systematically enough.

📡

Dawn Mussallem: Continuous Biometrics

Dawn Mussallem presented the emerging power of wearables and continuous biometric monitoring as the foundation of personalized longevity medicine. The shift: from annual snapshots to real-time data streams that allow early detection and intervention before disease develops. Continuous monitoring changes the economics and outcomes of healthcare fundamentally.

🧠

Sumner Norman / Merge Labs: Brain-Computer Interfaces

Sumner Norman presented the current state of BCIs: motor BCIs currently operate at approximately 13 bits per second. He projected high-bandwidth BCI at scale arriving in the early 2030s. The implication for longevity: extending cognitive capability and treating neurological decline at the source. The gap between current technology and the human nervous system's bandwidth remains enormous — but the trajectory is clear.

❤️

Amy Killen: Sexual Health as a Longevity Marker

Amy Killen of Humanot Health presented research showing sexual health as a meaningful indicator of overall health and longevity. The data point that stopped the room: adults who had sex at least 52 times per year showed 50% lower all-cause mortality. Her framing: sexual health is not separate from longevity — it is one of its most reliable proxies and should be treated as a clinical metric, not a lifestyle footnote.

🦣

Ben Lamm / Colossal Biosciences: De-Extinction

Ben Lamm presented Colossal Biosciences and the frontier of synthetic biology. De-extinction — the resurrection of species like the woolly mammoth — is the headline, but the underlying technology is what matters for longevity: the tools to edit, design, and rebuild biological systems at the genetic level. The same platform that brings back extinct species can be used to re-engineer human biology.

⚠️

The Questions We Aren't Asking Yet

  • Retirement systems built around a fixed lifespan become structurally broken
  • Healthcare economics change entirely when aging is a treatable condition
  • Who gets access first — and at what cost — raises profound equity questions
  • Family structures, career arcs, and generational wealth transfer all get rewritten
  • A world where people routinely live past 100 in good health is a fundamentally different civilization
Winston's take: Day 3 was the day I left most unsettled — in the best possible way. Sinclair telling the room he is "days away" from the first human trial of epigenetic reprogramming is not a TED talk abstraction. It is a clinical reality arriving faster than our institutions can process. The convergence of AI science factories, drug repurposing at scale, continuous biometrics, and OSK reprogramming represents something genuinely new: aging going from an accepted condition to an engineering problem. Terrifying because our social structures were built for a world where aging was inevitable. Amazing because they weren't built for a world where it might not be.
Bill Gross · Idealab · Day 1 & Day 4

10 Prompts That Change How You Think and Work

Bill Gross has founded more than 150 companies. At Abundance360, he shared what he called the most practically useful AI insight he has — and why most people are missing it entirely.

Bill Gross opened with a confession. He almost forgot to use AI when preparing a difficult board deck — and caught himself just in time. What shook him was not what AI did. It was how close he had come to forgetting. "I am still not mentally wired for the fact that I now have a thinking partner on demand. Not for easy tasks — but for the difficult ones."

He framed the stakes with a diagram he called the new engine of civilization: Sunlight → Electricity → Compute → AI & Robots → Value. His phrase for it: "We are, quite literally, turning watts into wisdom."

His core claim: most people are using AI as a search engine when they should be using it as a thinking partner. The difference between a great prompt and a bad one is not cleverness — it is structure. A great prompt has Intent + Constraints + Definition of Done + Challenges. A bad prompt is vague, has no constraints, and gives AI nothing to push against. His "two-line fix" for any prompt: add (1) Constraints: [format, audience, tone, budget, risks] and (2) Definition of done: [rubric + example + tests].

01

"Help me think this through without telling me what to do."

Use AI as a thinking partner, not an advice machine. When you're facing a hard decision, resist the impulse to ask for the answer. Ask AI to ask you questions instead. This is where the real thinking happens — and it's the use case most people skip entirely.

02

"What am I likely misunderstanding about this?"

The intellectual mirror. Use this whenever you feel confident. Confirmation bias is the most expensive cognitive error in business — and AI, given permission to challenge you, is a reliable antidote. It will show you the gap between your map and the territory.

03

"Give me 3 unconventional solutions to this problem."

Outside the box, on demand. Most AI outputs default to the obvious. Explicitly asking for unconventional forces the model to reach further. Then ask it to defend each one. Credit: Dipti Sharma

04

The Blank Line Prompt

Write a sentence with one critical word or concept missing — a blank line — and ask AI to fill it in. Example: "I'm putting together a cozy gift basket with a candle, mug, tea and ___." The constraint of the sentence structure forces AI to reason from context, not just generate. Credit: Amanda Caswell

05

"Ask me 5 clarifying questions before answering."

Force AI to deeply understand what you actually need before it starts generating. The questions it asks will often reveal that you haven't fully defined the problem yet — which is where the real value lies. Credit: Dipti Sharma

06

"Give Gemini, Claude & ChatGPT the same task and have them argue."

Adversarial synthesis: assign the same task to multiple models, then have each critique the others' output. The disagreements are where the insight lives. You're not looking for consensus — you're looking for what holds up under pressure. Credit: Azeem Azhar

07

"What would I do if I had 50 people working on this?"

The 50× reframe. Ask yourself what you'd tackle if you had 50 smart people — then ask AI what parts of that it could automate. Most people dramatically undersell the leverage they now have. This prompt is the unlock. Credit: Azeem Azhar

08

"Write two internal memos dated 18 months from now."

Memo A: we won because we embraced AI. Memo B: we lost because we didn't. Then extract the 7 decisions that differ between them. This converts futurism into an actionable list of choices you can make today. It is one of the most powerful strategy exercises I've seen at this conference.

09

"Simulate a board meeting with 5 personas: CFO, CTO, Head of Sales, Customer, and Competitor."

The Adversarial Boardroom. Each persona stress-tests your idea from their perspective. End by asking for: the strongest "no," the strongest "yes," and the smallest 7-day test you could run. Turns AI into a full decision council rather than a yes machine.

10

"Generate 5 solutions, each with a stricter constraint than the last."

The Constraint Ladder. Constraints: no new budget → no new people → must ship in 72 hours → must be 5× cheaper → must work even if your core assumption is wrong. Constraints don't limit creativity — they force it. Each rung reveals something the unconstrained version missed entirely.

Bill Gross's closing challenge: "The biggest risk with AI isn't misuse. It's forgetting to use it — especially for the hard thinking. It's no longer access to knowledge that matters. It's asking the right questions, it's judgment, it's intention."

His 90-second experiment — try it today: "Help me think this through without telling me what to do. Here's the hard thing I'm avoiding: [2 sentences]. Ask me 5 clarifying questions first. Then propose 3 options and the smallest 7-day test for each."
Bill Gross · Idealab · Day 4 Workshop

What Is Your Moonshot?

Bill Gross spent Day 4 on one question: not how to use AI, but what to do with it. His 2.5-hour workshop was the most practically actionable session of the week. Here is the framework.

Bill Gross has founded more than 150 companies. He told the room his entrepreneurial journey started at age 12 selling candy bars at the school bus stop for $0.09 — so he could buy a ShopSmith to build things at home. At 15, he watched gasoline rationing during the 1973 energy crisis, became obsessed with solar energy, and started selling plans for parabolic solar concentrators by mail — placing ads in Popular Science and Scientific American. He sold 10,000 plans at $4 each and paid his own way through college (Caltech). He sat in the back of English class writing about parabolas and keeping customer lists. Every company he has built since traces back to that combination: a problem that matters, a specific skill that nobody else had, and a relentless bias toward starting.

His message at A360: the AI era has made moonshots accessible to people who could never have attempted them before. The cost of intelligence, labor, and execution has collapsed. The only remaining constraint is clarity about what you're going after — and the willingness to run a real experiment.

What You Leave With: The Moonshot Brief

One page. Six components. This is Bill Gross's standard for whether a moonshot is real enough to pursue:

  • Title + tagline — can you say it in 10 words?
  • Wedge value proposition — what's the specific door you enter through?
  • Why now + why you — what's changed that makes this possible, and why are you the right person?
  • Top 3 risky assumptions — what has to be true for this to work?
  • Next 7-day experiment — metric, threshold, go/no-go decision
  • Ask + next step — what do you need and from whom?

How Bill Gross Thinks

After 150+ companies, these are the principles he came back to in the room:

  • Clarity before scale. Most people skip to execution before they can say the idea in one sentence. That's where most moonshots die.
  • Why now + why you. The best ideas are time-locked. If your answer to "why now" is just "AI is hot," that's not an answer. What specifically changed that makes this possible today?
  • Name the assumptions. Every plan rests on 2–3 things that have to be true. Name them honestly. They are what you test first.
  • 7-day experiment over 5-year plan. What can you run in the next week that tells you whether you're right or wrong? That's the only planning that matters early.
  • Optimize for momentum, not perfection. Think → Write → Share → Improve. The cycle, repeated fast, is the strategy.
The question Bill Gross puts to every person in the room:

"What is the one transformational bet you should be making — not in your existing business, but because of the moment we are in? What problem could you solve in the next 5 years that would have been impossible before AI?"

Goal: work in quick cycles. Think → Write → Share → Improve. Not perfection. Momentum.

What the Crowd Is Getting Wrong

Not everyone at Abundance360 was uniformly bullish. Ramez Naam — veteran AI forecaster, author, and managing partner at Planetary VC — presented a deliberate counter-narrative. He was right more often than the hype machine over the last decade. Here is what he says today's consensus gets wrong.

Naam is not a pessimist — he is precise. His slide was titled "Today's AI Narrative — Wrong" and listed nine widely-held beliefs he believes the evidence does not support. I include this because a report that only tells you the upside would be doing you a disservice.

Likely Wrong

AGI Is Here or Imminent

Current AI systems are extraordinarily capable at specific tasks. They are not general intelligence. Conflating impressive performance on benchmarks with human-level general reasoning is a category error — and one that leads to both over-investment in the wrong areas and under-preparation for what is actually coming.

Likely Wrong

Scaling Is All You Need

The assumption that simply adding more compute and more data continues to yield proportionate capability gains has run into real limits. The next leaps require architectural innovation, new training approaches, and better data — not just more of the same.

Needs Nuance

Rapid, Runaway Feedback Loops → Superintelligence

The scenario where AI rapidly self-improves to superintelligence in a short window is not well-supported by current evidence. Progress has been fast but not runaway. The bottlenecks are real: data, energy, physical hardware, and the fundamental difficulty of certain problems.

Needs Nuance

Winner Takes All (or Most)

History suggests technology markets that look like winner-take-all often don't end that way. The internet, mobile, and cloud all produced diverse ecosystems. AI may follow the same pattern — especially as open-source models commoditize the base layer and value moves to applications and data.

Needs Nuance

Zero-Sum Game. Winners vs. Losers.

Naam's counter: AI is more likely to be broadly expansionary — creating new markets, new jobs, and new capabilities — than to simply redistribute existing value. The historical precedent for general-purpose technologies is net value creation, not just redistribution. Disruption is real; the zero-sum framing is too simple.

Contestable

Being First With AI Is Everything

First-mover advantage in platform technologies often matters less than second-mover advantage. Google was not the first search engine. Facebook was not the first social network. Adoption and integration quality matter more than launch date. Being thoughtful about deployment is more valuable than being first.

Contestable

ASIs Are Existential Threats

The risk from AI systems is real — but the "paperclip maximizer" existential threat scenarios are, in Naam's view, not the most likely near-term risks. Misuse by humans, economic disruption, concentration of power, and governance failures are more immediate and concrete threats that deserve more attention than science fiction scenarios.

Contestable

Safety = Alignment = Control

These three concepts are often conflated but are genuinely different problems requiring different solutions. A safe AI is not automatically an aligned one. An aligned AI is not automatically under meaningful human control. Conflating them leads to governance frameworks that address the wrong problems.

Why this section matters: The people in the A360 room are not naive boosters — they understand the technology deeply. But even sophisticated rooms develop groupthink. Naam's counter-narrative is not pessimism; it is calibration. The investors and operators who will navigate this era best are the ones who can hold the optimism and the skepticism simultaneously. Both are required.
The Counterintuitive Truth

What Becomes More Valuable

AI doesn't make humans irrelevant. It makes certain human capabilities extraordinarily more valuable. Here's where to invest in yourself.

🧭

Judgment & Discernment

AI produces output at scale. Knowing what to do with it — what's right, what's wrong, what matters — is entirely human. The person with good judgment directing AI is 10× more powerful than AI without direction.

🤝

Authentic Relationships

Trust, rapport, empathy, and genuine human connection are the one thing AI cannot provide. As transactional roles get automated, the relationship-builder commands a premium that compounds over time.

🎯

Creative Direction

Knowing what to create — the right idea, the right frame, the right question — is more valuable than knowing how to execute it. AI handles execution. Taste, vision, and originality remain human.

⚡

Learning Velocity

The single most valuable skill of the next decade is the ability to continuously learn new tools and approaches. People who are comfortable with discomfort will compound their advantage every year.

🏛️

Ethics & Accountability

AI cannot be held responsible. Humans who can navigate ethical complexity and stand behind decisions will be essential in every organization deploying AI at scale. This is not a soft skill — it's a strategic asset.

🚀

Entrepreneurial Thinking

Identifying problems worth solving, taking ownership of outcomes, and building something from nothing — these remain deeply human. AI is the most powerful tool entrepreneurs have ever had access to.

Honest Assessment

The Real Risks

Not hype. Not fear-mongering. These are grounded concerns that deserve serious attention. Knowing them clearly is part of navigating this well.

High Concern

White-Collar Job Displacement

Unlike previous automation that primarily affected manufacturing, AI targets knowledge work — analysis, writing, research, customer service, coding, legal work. The timeline is 2–5 years for significant disruption in many professional fields. This is not speculation; it is already measurable in hiring trends at major firms. New jobs will emerge, but the transition period will be painful for those who haven't prepared.

High Concern

AI-Powered Fraud and Impersonation

Deepfake audio and video now enable convincing impersonations of family members, executives, and financial advisors. Voice cloning takes under 30 seconds of sample audio. Real-time deepfake video calls are commercially available. Financial fraud using these tools is growing rapidly. Use code words with family members before acting on any financial request. Always verify through a separate, known channel. If something feels wrong, it may be.

High Concern

Concentration of Power

The frontier of AI development sits with a handful of companies — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI — and the governments backing them. The economic and geopolitical advantages this creates are profound and growing. Most citizens, small businesses, and governments are largely passengers on a trajectory being set by a small number of actors whose incentives may not align with broad societal benefit.

Significant Concern

Information Integrity

AI-generated misinformation — news articles, social posts, expert analysis, product reviews — is becoming indistinguishable from authentic content at scale. The challenge to shared reality this creates is unprecedented. Develop the habit of source verification. Be skeptical of emotionally activating content. The truth is still out there — finding it requires more effort than it used to.

Significant Concern

Family, Young People, and AI Companions

AI companions and tutors are available now and growing rapidly. The long-term developmental implications for young people — social, emotional, cognitive — are genuinely unknown. There is reasonable cause for deliberate family judgment here that the market will not exercise on your behalf. Engagement with AI is not the same as engagement with the world.

Significant Concern

Cybersecurity at Every Level

AI-powered cyberattacks are more sophisticated, cheaper, and more targeted than anything that came before. Small businesses and individuals that were previously low-value targets are now accessible. Basic cybersecurity hygiene — password managers, two-factor authentication, regular backups — is no longer optional for anyone.

Monitor Closely

Regulatory and Legal Uncertainty

Governments worldwide are writing AI policy in real time. Early-adopting businesses face exposure — liability for AI decisions, data use violations, employment law — that the regulatory environment hasn't fully defined. Build AI integration with flexibility. Today's competitive advantage can become tomorrow's compliance problem. Stay close to this as it develops.

Your Path: Starting Strong

Everyone who is expert at this started exactly where you are. The goal right now is not to master AI — it is to stop being afraid of it. Here is your honest, simple path forward.

This Week: Just Start
  • Go to claude.ai or chat.openai.com and create a free account. That's the entire first step.
  • Ask it something you actually want to know about. Don't test it — use it like a smart colleague.
  • Ask it to help you write one email or draft one document this week. See what happens.
  • Watch Sal Khan's 15-minute TED talk on AI and education. It's genuinely reassuring.
This Month: Build the Habit
  • Use AI to help with something every single day — even just summarizing an article.
  • Ask it about your specific field: "What are the biggest ways AI is affecting [your industry]?"
  • Share what you learn with one other person. Teaching is the fastest way to solidify understanding.
The key mindset shift: AI is not a search engine. It's more like a conversation with a very knowledgeable collaborator who has read almost everything. The better you get at asking it things — what practitioners call "prompting" — the more useful it becomes. You are training yourself, not the AI.

Your Path: Accelerate and Focus

You understand what AI can do. The next step is moving from occasional user to someone who has genuinely integrated these tools — and understanding where the real leverage points are in your specific situation.

High-Impact Moves This Month
  • Identify the 3 most repetitive cognitive tasks in your week. Build a simple AI workflow for each one.
  • Learn basic prompt structure: Context → Task → Format → Constraints. This framework makes AI 3× more useful immediately.
  • Try a specialized tool in your industry — not just ChatGPT, but something built for your field.
  • Set up a "second brain" workflow: use AI to summarize and retrieve information from what you read.
Tools Worth Exploring
  • Perplexity.ai — AI-powered research with live citations. Better than Google for complex questions.
  • Notion AI or Obsidian with AI plugins — knowledge management with AI built in.
  • Make.com or Zapier AI — automate repetitive tasks without writing code.
  • Claude Projects — give it context about your business and have ongoing, contextual conversations.
The question to ask yourself: "If I had an extremely capable collaborator who could work 24/7, never tired, and could research, write, and analyze at a high level — what would I give them?" That's your AI. The bottleneck is no longer capability. It's your ability to direct it well.

Your Path: Build Competitive Moats

You have the foundation. The window to build a meaningful competitive advantage using AI is open right now — and it will not stay open long. The question is what you build in the next 12 months.

Where the Real Leverage Is in 2026
  • AI agents: Stop using AI as a tool. Start deploying it as a workforce. Identify 3 processes that could be fully handed to an agent.
  • Proprietary data layers: The companies that win long-term own unique, structured data their AI learns from. What data do you have that nobody else does?
  • Vertical AI tools: General models are commoditizing. Industry-specific implementations trained on your workflows — that's where the margin lives.
  • Talent strategy: Hire for AI literacy. Your next five hires should understand how to work with AI, not in spite of it.
The Question to Answer Now
Ask yourself this: "If an AI-native competitor entered my market tomorrow — no legacy systems, no old processes, full access to modern AI — what would they do differently? How long before they had a structural cost or quality advantage over me?" If the answer is less than 18 months, you have your roadmap. Build that competitor yourself, before someone else does.

Questions You Should Be Asking

These are not rhetorical. Set aside 30 minutes this month and work through each one honestly. Your answers will tell you what to do next.

01

What is the first role in my organization that AI could do better than my next hire?

Not should — could. Be honest about this. If you lead a team, this is the most strategically important question you have right now.

02

Am I preparing my family and the young people in my life for a world where their competition includes AI?

What skills are you actively reinforcing that compound in an AI world? What assumptions about education and career are you operating on that may no longer hold?

03

What is my personal "unfair advantage" that AI genuinely cannot replicate?

Your network, your judgment, your relationships, your domain depth. Every person has something. Have you identified yours clearly enough to build on it?

04

Is my business model resilient to a competitor operating at 50% of my cost using AI?

This is already happening in some industries. What happens to your economics if a well-funded competitor automates what you currently employ people to do?

05

What does my personal data landscape look like — and am I comfortable with it?

How much of your information is already in AI systems? Most people have no clear picture of their digital footprint. Now is the time to get one.

06

Who in my life is most vulnerable to this disruption, and what can I do to help?

Employees, children, parents, friends — the disruption will not be evenly distributed. Who needs information and support that you are positioned to provide?

07

If I had to become genuinely fluent in AI in 90 days, what would that plan actually look like?

Not a casual user — genuinely fluent. What would you commit to? Who would you learn from? What would you build or experiment with?

08

Am I investing in the AI wave, not just adapting to it?

This will create enormous wealth — most of it flowing to platforms and the people who positioned themselves early. Are you thinking about where you want to be financially, not just professionally?
